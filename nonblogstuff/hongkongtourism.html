Hong Kong Tourism Ideas

Last updated: April 20, 2026

I'm assuming you're an international traveller staying for anywhere from 1-3 nights. These are my personal views only.

Sightseeing

Star Ferry — this is a must-do. You take the upper deck and enjoy the view. People generally go from Tsim Sha Tsui to Central and vice versa. The Wan Chai route is no good because the Wan Chai terminal has nothing good nearby, don't take that one.

The Peak — many people say the Hong Kong skyline looks good. Best enjoyed in afternoon or at night. Lugard Road is the best place to see it. Take the bus or the minibus down, instead of the tram; the ride is exciting. You can take the tram up if you must.

Tsim Sha Tsui promenade — this is good too. More crowded, more tourists. Night-time is the best time to go. The view is different because you're on the other side of the harbour.

Wong Tai Sin temple — an authentic yet tourist-friendly temple. They do all that temple stuff there. Traditional vibes.

Trams on Hong Kong Island — you can take a tram and enjoy the view. They're old school and very slow.

Noah's Ark — you can visit a life-size recreation of Noah's Ark, which also includes an explanation of the theory of evolution.

Shopping

Temple Street night market — very touristy, but it's pretty fun to visit and get your fortune told or buy some stuff.

Mong Kok ladies' market — also touristy, but they sell interesting stuff that's either too fashionable or unwanted by most people. Very conveniently located and easy to slot in to an itinerary.

Cat Street and Antique Street markets — this is less touristy, but more of a tourist trap. Hard to explain. It feels nice to walk along and the vibe is really relaxed, even though objectively the shops aren't very interesting. I still like it.

Food

Comparatively, the most interesting food is non-dim sum Cantonese food, seafood, Hong Kong style borscht soup, and Cantonese desserts. The Cantonese desserts generally don't taste good but it's worth trying once. Dim sum is good, but not better than what you'd find in Western cities with large Chinese populations.

Cha Chaan Teng — local diners that serve food like strong milk tea, lemon tea, tea with coffee, macaroni with ham, pineapple buns with butter, French toast, satay beef vermicelli noodles. Some people like this stuff I guess. Also borscht soup. I like the borscht soup the most.

Tuen Mun Sam Shing seafood — you buy the seafood from the tanks and then take the live seafood to a restaurant where they cook it for you. It's inconvenient so you want to take a taxi to and fro, and it's only worth it if you're a foodie. You want the mantis prawns and the steamed scallops with vermicelli. They have similar things in Lei Yue Mun and Sai Kung if you want more convenient but not-as-good seafood.

Cantonese seafood restaurants — I like the steamed fish the most, but people say it's an acquired taste. Just try anything you want, you can't really go wrong.

Sham Shui Po dessert places — Sham Shui Po is a working-class district so not many people speak English. You can just use Google Translate or something. Everything is fairly good.

Nightlife

Don't ask me, I wouldn't know. How did you even find this blogpost if you're a twenty year old party animal?

Nature, hiking, watersports

I don't think it's worth it. This kind of stuff is more for local residents. They say the Dragon's Back hiking trail is really good, so if you like outdoors activities, you could check that out. Some people like taking the ferry to Lamma Island or Cheung Chau for a day trip to hike, too.

Getting around

You want to buy an Octopus card ASAP to get around, because all the public transport uses it. MTRs, taxis, and buses are probably the most useful transport options, in that order.

Accommodations

You probably want to stay either somewhere on Hong Kong Island or in Kowloon. Don't get something too cheap. A 4 star hotel is the sweet spot. It varies too much to give any useful advice, just book something that's conveniently located.
